Creative Vision

Light, Land, and Story

Philo Ridge Farm is not a place that needs embellishment. The work speaks for itself: hands in the soil, animals moving across open pasture, a kitchen that honors what the land provides, and a market stocked with products that celebrate Vermont's local artisans, producers, and farmers. My approach is to let the farm's own rhythms guide the camera, capturing the authentic texture of each day with patience and care.

I work with a documentary-inspired approach that combines efficient production with an eye for unmistakable verite moments. Working with natural light and clean composition, I've developed a sharp instinct for the decisive moment, knowing when stillness serves the story and when to follow the action. No heavy color grading or manufactured drama, just rich, honest imagery that feels as rooted as the farm itself. The goal is footage that earns trust: when someone watches these films, they should feel like they've been there, and if they haven't, they will feel the need to visit.

Audio matters as much as image. I pay close attention to the environmental soundscape: wind through the pasture, the rhythmic thud of a kitchen knife, quiet conversation between teammates, woven into the edit as a way of grounding the viewer in place. Interviews will be conversational and relaxed, drawing out the genuine voices and vision behind the work.

Having worked as a cinematographer, director, and editor for companies such as PBS, HBO, and NBC gives me the ability to always be shooting for the edit. I see the frame and the transcript at the same time, making mental notes as I go, tracking the moments that will matter in post. That fluency across all three disciplines means I can take an interview from raw footage to transcript to a seamlessly distilled sequence of its best moments, quickly and without losing what made it real.

Creative Treatment

Five Stories, One Farm

Each short film is a window into one facet of Philo Ridge, self-contained but sharing a unified visual language and pace. Here's how I see each one unfolding:

Film 01

Market Garden

Dawn light on rows of greens. Hands thinning seedlings. Water catching light in the greenhouse. This piece moves at the pace of growing things: slow, deliberate, seasonal. We follow the arc of a single morning's harvest from soil to crate, letting the work tell the story. The market carries a calm, familiar sense of community and health, a place where neighbors know each other and trust what's on the table. That trust runs deep, grounded in a commitment to organic sustainability that shapes everything from soil practices to the partnerships Philo Ridge cultivates with like-minded food producers who share the same values. The result is a carefully observed and thoughtfully captured celebration of seasonal ingredients, rooted in a deep ecological commitment.

Film 02

Livestock & Land Stewardship

Cattle in open pasture. Sheep moving through rotational paddocks. The close, trusting relationship between animals and the people who tend them. Wide aerial shots reveal the scale and intentionality of the land management, while intimate close-ups show the daily care that makes it work. A documentary filmmaking approach will capture the heart of Philo Ridge as it truly is: an authentic vision of grass farmers tending fields of diverse grasses, forbs, and legumes within a perennial pasture system. The camera follows rotational grazing as it unfolds across the landscape, revealing the deliberate, patient work that builds soil health and sustains the farm's remarkable biodiversity.

Film 03

Food & Hospitality

The kitchen as continuation of the field. Steam rising, knives working through just-picked vegetables, the quiet choreography of a professional kitchen. We trace the thread from harvest to plate and capture the dining experience as it's meant to be felt: warm, unhurried, connected to the land. My previous experience filming cooking television shows will bring an instinctive eye to documenting Chef Marc St. Jacques as he crafts menus that highlight the organic produce and pasture-raised meats grown right here on the farm. Carefully crafted lighting that precisely replicates natural light brings out the earthy colors and textures of each dish, honoring the connection between the land, the farmer, and the plate.

Film 04

Field Research

Hands collecting soil samples. Quiet focus in the field. The intellectual engine behind the farm's regenerative practices. This film positions Philo Ridge as a place where farming and science meet: curious, rigorous, forward-looking. Interviews with researchers anchor the narrative in purpose. My background in Forest Biology and field work informs a verite sensibility ideally suited to this subject. The camera moves through the Philo Ridge ecosystem with genuine understanding, finding the visual language to make IPM practices, water quality, and biodiversity not just legible but compelling. Science elevated through imagery, so that what matters most lands with both clarity and beauty.

Film 05

Classes & Events

The farm as gathering place. Whether it's a workshop on soil health, a farm tour, or a speaker series, this film captures the energy and community that Philo Ridge builds around its mission. A decade of documentary wedding photography, with commissions throughout New England and for special events in California, Sweden, and France, honed a particular skill: the ability to enter a charged moment and capture it in a way that feels genuinely magical without disturbing it. That instinct translates directly here. Candid moments of learning, laughter, and shared meals tell the story of a farm that's as much about people as it is about land.

About

Corey Hendrickson

My connection to farm life goes back further than my career does. I spent my earliest years on a farm in New Hampshire, the memories are hazy, but the imprint wasn't. Some experiences shape you quietly, and without fully understanding why, I have always felt most at home in working landscapes, most comfortable up to my knees in muck with animals chewing on my shirt and sniffing my camera gear.

This love of working lands fully germinated during my high school summers while picking vegetables at Wilson Farms in Massachusetts, where I learned early what it actually means to do hard work.

I went on to study Forestry at the University of Vermont, which led to two seasons in Alaska as a field researcher for the US Forest Service, followed by several more summers fighting wildfire with the USFS Pike Hotshots across the Western United States. Long days in demanding and unpredictable conditions became second nature, and it turned out to be perfect training for the endurance and adaptability that film production requires.

Over the past 20 years I have been filming and photographing on farms across the region, developing a visual language for agricultural life that goes beyond the picturesque. I understand how farms actually work, which means I know where to be, when to wait, and when to move.

That experience runs alongside my work as a producer, director, and editor for a nationally aired, award-winning PBS series based on New England life, work that taught me how to find and shape a compelling story, manage complex productions, and deliver results that meet the highest broadcast standards. My cinematography has been contracted by PBS, HBO and NBC.

I also bring extensive experience creating still photography in conjunction with video production, allowing for a cohesive vision across both deliverables. This skillset has been harnessed by national brands, as well as organizations and non-profits throughout Vermont. I have been a contributing photographer to Yankee Magazine for nearly two decades, capturing all facets of life in rural New England from portraits to food to travel stories.

For Philo Ridge Farm, I bring all of it: the technical craft, the storytelling instincts, and a genuine feel for the rhythms of farm life that no amount of research can replicate.
